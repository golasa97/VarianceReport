\begin{abstract}
Properly accounting for resonance self-shielding in the Unresolved Resonance Region (URR) is critical for accurate neutron transport simulation. The standard method relies on probability tables (p-tables) to represent the statistical variance of the cross-section. This paper identifies a methodological flaw in the standard p-table generation and application pipeline, wherein a decoupling of mean cross-section values leads to a non-physical scaling of the intended variance. This scaling introduces significant errors in self-shielding calculations. We derive a robust linear transformation to correct this flaw, ensuring the scaled probability distribution preserves the original, physically-sampled variance while matching the required infinitely-dilute mean. This corrected methodology is validated against simple transmission problems and benchmark applications for ${}^{235}$U, demonstrating a complete correction of the self-shielding error.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The energy-dependent structure of neutron cross-sections is categorized into distinct regions. In between the Resolved Resonance Region (RRR) and the continuum region lies the Unresolved Resonance Region (URR), where cross-sections possess resonance structure, but individual resonances cannot be resolved experimentally. URR evaluations are thus limited to average cross-sections and average resonance parameters. This representation, however, omits the large variance in the cross-section's magnitude, which introduces a critical non-linear relationship between the average cross-section and the average neutron transmission. This effect is known as resonance self-shielding.

The standard method to account for self-shielding is the use of probability tables. These tables provide a discrete probability distribution of cross-section values, generated by statistically sampling many resonance "ladders" to reflect the underlying variance. It is critical that this variance is preserved.

This paper identifies a loss of variance preservation arising from a subtle decoupling in the standard methodology. Probability tables are generated by normalizing sampled resonance ladders against their own statistical average cross-section, but transport codes often sample from these tables using a different average cross-section (the smooth, infinitely-dilute value). When a discrepancy exists between these two averages, the variance is inadvertently scaled, leading to incorrect self-shielding. This paper will demonstrate this problem, derive a corrective methodology, and validate it using ${}^{235}$U test cases.

\section{Theory and Background}

To clarify the notation used throughout this section, the following terms are defined. The true, unresolved cross-section is denoted by $\sigma$, which is statistically sampled to generate resonance ladders; its Monte Carlo samples are $\sigma_i$ with a sample mean of $\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}$. The deterministic, infinitely-dilute cross-section from the nuclear data evaluation (e.g., File 3) is denoted by $\sigma_{eval}(E)$, and its bin-averaged value is $\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}$. Finally, the cross-section values from the probability table, as used in the transport code, are denoted by $\sigma_{PT}$.

\subsection{Accounting for Self-Shielding}

For a known cross-section $\sigma(E)$, the true average transmission $\langle T \rangle_{true}$ over an energy bin $\Delta E$ is:
\begin{equation}
\langle T \rangle_{true} = \frac{1}{\Delta E} \int_{E_1}^{E_2} e^{-n\sigma(E)} dE
\label{eq:true_T}
\end{equation}
In the URR, only the average cross-section, $\langle \sigma \rangle$, is known. By adding and subtracting $\langle \sigma \rangle$ in the exponent, the terms can be separated:

\begin{align}
\langle T \rangle_{true} &= \frac{1}{\Delta E} \int_{E_1}^{E_2} e^{-n[\sigma(E) - \langle \sigma \rangle + \langle \sigma \rangle]} dE \\
&= e^{-n\langle \sigma \rangle} \left[ \frac{1}{\Delta E} \int_{E_1}^{E_2} e^{-n[\sigma(E) - \langle \sigma \rangle]} dE \right]
\label{eq:sep_T}
\end{align}
The $e^{-n\langle \sigma \rangle}$ term is the transmission based on the simple average. The integral term is the self-shielding contribution, $C_T$, which accounts for the variance.

\subsection{Background on Direct Sampling}
The self-shielding factor $C_T$ can be approximated via Monte Carlo integration:
\begin{equation}
C_T = \frac{1}{\Delta E} \int_{E_1}^{E_2} e^{-n[\sigma(E) - \langle \sigma \rangle]} dE \approx \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} e^{-n[\sigma_i - \langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}]}
\label{eq:CT_mc}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_i$ are cross-sections sampled from $N$ random resonance ladders, and $\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}$ is the statistical average of that sample set. The Monte Carlo averaged transmission is $\langle T \rangle_{MC}$. These are used to solve for $C_T$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\langle T \rangle_{MC}}{e^{-n\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}} \approx C_T
\label{eq:CT_solve}
\end{equation}
This $C_T$ term accurately captures the variance. However, $\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}$ may not be the best representation of the true infinitely-dilute average. Therefore, a different value, $\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}$, is substituted to get the final "ground truth" transmission:
\begin{equation}
\langle T \rangle_{true} \approx e^{-n\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}} C_T
\label{eq:T_ground_truth}
\end{equation}

By combining these two components:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The transmission of the smooth average: $\exp{(-n \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval})}$
    \item The self-shielding due to cross-section variance: $C_T$
\end{enumerate}
Then the self-shielded transmission is accurately approximated from average parameters and the average cross-section.

\subsection{Background on P-Table Calculations}
Transport codes cannot perform this sampling on the fly. Instead, p-tables are generated from the $N$ samples. These tables store probabilities $P_j$ and normalization factors $f_j$:
\begin{equation}
f_j = \frac{\sigma_j}{\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}
\label{eq:f_def}
\end{equation}
In the transport code, these factors are then used to construct the p-table cross-sections, $\sigma_{PT,j}$, by scaling with the "correct" infinitely-dilute cross-section, $\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}$:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{PT,j} = f_j \times \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}
\label{eq:sigma_prime}
\end{equation}
This ensures the mean of the sampled distribution is correct:
\begin{align}
    \langle \sigma_{PT} \rangle 
        &= \sum P_j \sigma_{PT,j} \nonumber \\
        &= \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval} \sum P_j f_j \nonumber \\
        &= \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval} \nonumber
\end{align}

\subsection{The Flaw: Unintended Variance Scaling}
This scaling procedure has an unintended consequence. The variance of the new distribution, $\text{Var}(\sigma_{PT})$, is scaled. This is shown by propagating the constant factor multiplicative property of variance, $\text{Var}(aX) = a^2 \text{Var}(X)$:
\begin{align}
\text{Var}(\sigma_{PT}) &= \text{Var}(f_j \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}) \\
&= \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}^2 \text{Var}(f_j) \\
&= \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}^2 \text{Var}\left(\frac{\sigma}{\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}\right) \\
&= \left(\frac{\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}}{\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}\right)^2 \text{Var}(\sigma)
\label{eq:var_scaling}
\end{align}
This equation shows that as $\frac{\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}}{\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}$ diverges from 1, the variance of the cross-section seen by the transport code varies by the square of that fraction, and the impact from self-shielding is incorrectly estimated.

\section{Variance-Preserving P-Table Generation}

\subsection{Bin-Averaged Approximation}
The first case considers a scenario where the infinitely-dilute cross-section, $\sigma_{eval}(E)$, exhibits negligible variance within the energy bin and can be represented by its average, $\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}$. The goal is to generate a set of p-table cross-sections $\sigma_{PT,j}$ satisfying two constraints:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Mean Constraint:} $E[\sigma_{PT}] = \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}$.
    \item \textbf{Variance Preservation:} $\var(\sigma_{PT}) = \var(\sigma)$.
\end{enumerate}

These constraints are satisfied by applying a constant additive shift to the original sampled distribution $\sigma_j$. The shift equates to the difference between the target mean and the sampled mean:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_{PT,j} = \sigma_j + (\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval} - \langle \sigma \rangle_{MC})
\end{equation}
This operation aligns the distribution mean to $\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}$ without altering the variance.

Expressing this in terms of the probability table normalization factor $f_j'$, defined by $\sigma_{PT,j} = f_j' \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}$, yields:
\begin{equation}
f_j' = \frac{\sigma_j + \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval} - \langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}{\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}}
\label{eq:f_prime_simple}
\end{equation}
This factor represents the original distribution shifted to the target mean, preserving the statistical variance structure.

\subsection{Intra-Bin Variance Correction}
The second case addresses scenarios where the infinitely-dilute cross-section, $\sigma_{eval}(E)$, exhibits significant point-wise fluctuations within the energy bin. The goal is to construct a new p-table distribution, $\sigma_{PT,j}(E)$, that correctly combines the energy-dependent mean of $\sigma_{eval}(E)$ with the statistical fluctuations from the resonance ladders. This is achieved by applying a linear transformation to the original sampled cross-sections, $\sigma_j$, to create a new random variable, $\sigma'_{PT,j}$, that satisfies specific constraints on its mean and variance.

The transformation is assumed to be of the form:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma'_{PT,j} = a \cdot \sigma_j + b
\end{equation}
The coefficients $a$ (scaling) and $b$ (shifting) are determined by enforcing the variance and mean constraints, respectively.

The scaling factor $a$ is determined by requiring that the variance of the new stochastic component, $\var(\sigma'_{PT})$, accounts for the portion of the total variance not explained by the evaluated trend, $\var(\sigma_{eval})$. The total variance from the resonance ladders, $\var(\sigma_{true})$, is split into two terms :
\begin{equation}
    \var(\sigma_{true}) = \var(\sigma_{eval}) + \var(\sigma'_{PT})
\end{equation}
This defines the target variance for the new component:
\begin{equation}
\var(\sigma'_{PT}) = \var(\sigma_{true}) - \var(\sigma_{eval})
\end{equation}

Next, this target is related to the original variance using the properties of the variance operator. Applying the variance operator to the linear transformation gives:
\begin{equation}
    \var(\sigma'_{PT}) = \var(a \cdot \sigma_j + b)
\end{equation}
Using the variance rules $\var(X+C) = \var(X)$ and $\var(CX) = C^2\var(X)$, this simplifies to:
\begin{equation}
    \var(\sigma'_{PT}) = a^2 \var(\sigma_j) = a^2 \var(\sigma_{true})
\end{equation}
By setting this equal to the target variance, the scaling factor $a$ can be solved for:
\begin{align}
    a^2 \var(\sigma_{true}) 
        &= \var(\sigma_{true}) - \var(\sigma_{eval}) \nonumber \\ \\
    a^2 
        &= \frac{\var(\sigma_{true}) - \var(\sigma_{eval})}{\var(\sigma_{true})} \nonumber \\
        &= 1 - \frac{\var(\sigma_{eval})}{\var(\sigma_{true})} \nonumber \\
    a 
        &= \sqrt{1 - \frac{\var(\sigma_{eval})}{\var(\sigma_{true})}}
\end{align}

The shifting factor $b$ is determined by requiring that the mean of the final distribution matches the target mean from the evaluation, $\sigma_{eval}(E)$. The final cross-section is $\sigma_{PT,j}(E) = \sigma_{eval}(E) + \sigma'_{PT,j}$. For the mean to be correct, the expectation of the stochastic component $\sigma'_{PT,j}$ must be zero.
\begin{equation}
    \langle \sigma'_{PT} \rangle = \langle a \cdot \sigma_j + b \rangle = a \langle \sigma_j \rangle + b = a \langle \sigma \rangle_{MC} + b
\end{equation}
Setting this to zero and solving for $b$ yields:
\begin{equation}
    b = -a \langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}
\end{equation}

Substituting the derived coefficients $a$ and $b$ back into the linear transformation gives the expression for the new stochastic component:
\begin{align}
    \sigma'_{PT,j} &= a \cdot \sigma_j - a \langle \sigma \rangle_{MC} \nonumber \\
                   &= a(\sigma_j - \langle \sigma \rangle_{MC})
\end{align}

The final reconstructed cross-section at any energy $E$ is the sum of the deterministic mean and this scaled, centered fluctuation:
\begin{align}
    \sigma_{PT,j}(E) 
        &= \sigma_{eval}(E) + \sigma'_{PT,j} \nonumber \\
        &= \sigma_{eval}(E) + a(\sigma_j - \langle \sigma \rangle_{MC})
\end{align}
Substituting the expression for $a$ gives the final form:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{PT,j}(E) = \sigma_{eval}(E) + \left[ \sqrt{1 - \frac{\var(\sigma_{eval})}{\var(\sigma_{true})}} \right] (\sigma_j - \langle \sigma \rangle_{MC})
\end{equation}

This formulation ensures that the mean of the distribution at any energy $E$ is $\sigma_{eval}(E)$ and that the total variance over the bin is preserved.

\subsubsection{Relationship Between P-Table Models}
This final, most general formulation encompasses the other two methodologies discussed. It can be seen as a correction that accounts for two distinct physical effects: the discrepancy between the sampled and evaluated mean cross-sections, and the potential for "double counting" variance when the evaluated cross-section itself has intra-bin structure.

The relationship to the bin-averaged approximation is clear. In the case where the evaluated cross-section has negligible variance within the bin ($\var(\sigma_{eval}) \to 0$), the scaling factor $a$ approaches 1:
\begin{equation}
    a = \sqrt{1 - \frac{\var(\sigma_{eval})}{\var(\sigma_{true})}} \to \sqrt{1 - 0} = 1
\end{equation}
The general equation then simplifies to the bin-averaged form, where the fluctuation is simply shifted to the new mean:
\begin{equation}
    \sigma_{PT,j}(E) = \sigma_{eval}(E) + (\sigma_j - \langle \sigma \rangle_{MC})
\end{equation}
If $\sigma_{eval}(E)$ is approximated by its bin-average $\langle\sigma\rangle_{eval}$, this is identical to the expression derived in the bin-averaged case.

Furthermore, this bin-averaged form itself relates back to the original methodology. If the statistical mean of the resonance ladders happens to perfectly match the evaluated mean ($\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC} \to \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}$), the additive shift becomes zero, and the corrected cross-section becomes $\sigma_{PT,j} \to \sigma_j$. Under this same condition, the original multiplicative scaling, $\sigma_{PT,j} = (\sigma_j / \langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}) \times \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}$, also reduces to $\sigma_{PT,j} \to \sigma_j$. Consequently, the derived transformations are generalizations that correctly handle cases where these idealized conditions are not met.

\subsection{Data Preparation}
The simulations were performed using ACE files generated from the ENDF/B-VIII.1 nuclear data library. For the ${}^{235}U$ isotope, two distinct ACE files were created using the NJOY nuclear data processing code. The first file was generated using the standard methodology for unresolved resonance region calculations with the `LSSF=1` option, which applies a multiplicative scaling to the probability tables. This approach is detailed in \cite{ENDF369}. The second file was generated using a modified approach where the variance-preserving additive transformation derived in this paper was applied to the probability table bins. All other isotopes were processed using the standard NJOY pathway.

This systematic impact highlights the importance of the correction for accurate benchmark calculations.

\begin{abstract}
Properly accounting for resonance self-shielding in the Unresolved Resonance Region (URR) is critical for accurate neutron transport simulation. The standard method relies on probability tables (p-tables) to represent the statistical variance of the cross-section. This paper identifies a methodological flaw in the standard p-table generation and application pipeline, wherein a decoupling of mean cross-section values leads to a non-physical scaling of the intended variance. This scaling introduces significant errors in self-shielding calculations. We derive a robust linear transformation to correct this flaw, ensuring the scaled probability distribution preserves the original, physically-sampled variance while matching the required infinitely-dilute mean. This corrected methodology is validated against simple transmission problems and benchmark applications for ${}^{235}U$, demonstrating a complete correction of the self-shielding error.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The energy-dependent structure of neutron cross-sections is categorized into distinct regions. In between the Resolved Resonance Region (RRR) and the continuum region lies the Unresolved Resonance Region (URR), where cross-sections possess resonance structure, but individual resonances cannot be resolved experimentally. URR evaluations are thus limited to average cross-sections and average resonance parameters. This representation, however, omits the large variance in the cross-section's magnitude, which introduces a critical non-linear relationship between the average cross-section and the average neutron transmission. This effect is known as resonance self-shielding.

The standard method to account for self-shielding is the use of probability tables. These tables provide a discrete probability distribution of cross-section values, generated by statistically sampling many resonance "ladders" to reflect the underlying variance. It is critical that this variance is preserved.

This paper identifies a loss of variance preservation arising from a subtle decoupling in the standard methodology. Probability tables are generated by normalizing sampled resonance ladders against their own statistical average cross-section, but transport codes often sample from these tables using a different average cross-section (the smooth, infinitely-dilute value). When a discrepancy exists between these two averages, the variance is inadvertently scaled, leading to incorrect self-shielding. This paper will demonstrate this problem, derive a corrective methodology, and validate it using ${}^{235}U$ test cases.

\section{Theory and Background}

\subsection{Accounting for Self-Shielding}

For a known cross-section $\sigma(E)$, the true average transmission $\langle T \rangle_{true}$ over an energy bin $\Delta E$ is:
\begin{equation}
\langle T \rangle_{true} = \frac{1}{\Delta E} \int_{E_1}^{E_2} e^{-n\sigma(E)} dE
\label{eq:true_T}
\end{equation}
In the URR, we only know the average cross-section, $\langle \sigma \rangle$. By adding and subtracting $\langle \sigma \rangle$ in the exponent, we can separate the terms:
\begin{align}
\langle T \rangle_{true} &= \frac{1}{\Delta E} \int_{E_1}^{E_2} e^{-n[\sigma(E) - \langle \sigma \rangle + \langle \sigma \rangle]} dE \\
&= e^{-n\langle \sigma \rangle} \left[ \frac{1}{\Delta E} \int_{E_1}^{E_2} e^{-n[\sigma(E) - \langle \sigma \rangle]} dE \right]
\label{eq:sep_T}
\end{align}
The $e^{-n\langle \sigma \rangle}$ term is the transmission based on the simple average. The integral term is the self-shielding contribution, $C_T$, which accounts for the variance.

\subsection{Background on Direct Sampling}
The self-shielding factor $C_T$ can be approximated via Monte Carlo integration:
\begin{equation}
C_T = \frac{1}{\Delta E} \int_{E_1}^{E_2} e^{-n[\sigma(E) - \langle \sigma \rangle]} dE \approx \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} e^{-n[\sigma_i - \langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}]}
\label{eq:CT_mc}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_i$ are cross-sections sampled from $N$ random resonance ladders, and $\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}$ is the statistical average of that sample set. The Monte Carlo averaged transmission is $\langle T \rangle_{MC}$. These are used to solve for $C_T$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\langle T \rangle_{MC}}{e^{-n\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}} \approx C_T
\label{eq:CT_solve}
\end{equation}
This $C_T$ term accurately captures the variance. However, $\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}$ may not be the best representation of the true infinitely-dilute average. Therefore, a different value, $\langle \sigma' \rangle$, is substituted to get the final "ground truth" transmission:
\begin{equation}
\langle T \rangle_{true} \approx e^{-n\langle \sigma' \rangle} C_T
\label{eq:T_ground_truth}
\end{equation}

By combining these two components:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The transmission of the smooth average: $\exp{(-n \langle \sigma' \rangle)}$
    \item The self-shielding due to cross-section variance: $C_T$
\end{enumerate}
Then the self-shielded transmission is accurately approximated from average parameters and the average cross-section.

\subsection{Background on P-Table Calculations}
Transport codes cannot perform this sampling on the fly. Instead, p-tables are generated from the $N$ samples. These tables store probabilities $P_j$ and normalization factors $f_j$:
\begin{equation}
f_j = \frac{\sigma_j}{\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}
\label{eq:f_def}
\end{equation}
In the transport code, these factors are then scaled by the "correct" infinitely-dilute cross-section, $\langle \sigma' \rangle$:
\begin{equation}
\sigma_j' = f_j \times \langle \sigma' \rangle
\label{eq:sigma_prime}
\end{equation}
This ensures the mean of the sampled distribution is correct: $\langle \sigma' \rangle = \sum P_j \sigma_j' = \langle \sigma' \rangle \sum P_j f_j = \langle \sigma' \rangle$.

\subsection{The Flaw: Unintended Variance Scaling}
This scaling procedure has an unintended consequence. The variance of the new distribution, $\text{Var}(\sigma')$, is scaled. This is shown by propagating the constant factor multiplicative property of variance, $\text{Var}(aX) = a^2 \text{Var}(X)$:
\begin{align}
\text{Var}(\sigma') &= \text{Var}(f_j \langle \sigma' \rangle) \\
&= \langle \sigma' \rangle^2 \text{Var}(f_j) \\
&= \langle \sigma' \rangle^2 \text{Var}\left(\frac{\sigma}{\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}\right) \\
&= \left(\frac{\langle \sigma' \rangle}{\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}\right)^2 \text{Var}(\sigma)
\label{eq:var_scaling}
\end{align}
This equation shows that as $\frac{\langle \sigma' \rangle}{\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}$ diverges from 1, the variance of the cross-section seen by the transport code varies by the square of that fraction, and the impact from self-shielding is incorrectly estimated.

\section{Variance-Preserving P-Table Generation}

\subsection{The Objective and Constraints}
The objective is to find a transformation $f_j' = g(f_j, \avg{\sigma}, \avg{\sigma'})$ that generates a new set of normalization factors, $f_j'$. These factors must scale the original cross-section distribution (with mean $\avg{\sigma} = \sum P_j \sigma_j$) to a new target mean, $\avg{\sigma'}$, while strictly preserving the original variance.

This derivation is based on satisfying two constraints:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Mean Constraint:} The expectation value of the new normalization factors must be unity, ensuring $E[\sigma'] = \sum P_j (f_j' \avg{\sigma'}) = \avg{\sigma'}$. This implies:
    \begin{equation}
    E[f'] = \sum_{j} P_j f_j' = 1
    \label{eq:mean_constraint_f}
    \end{equation}
    
    \item \textbf{Variance Preservation:} The variance of the new, scaled distribution must equal the variance of the original: $\var(\sigma') = \var(\sigma)$.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Derivation of the Transformation}
A linear transformation is assumed for the function $g$, relating $f_j'$ to $f_j$ by two unknown constants, $a$ and $b$:
\begin{equation}
f_j' = a \cdot f_j + b
\label{eq:linear_assumption}
\end{equation}
The constants $a$ and $b$ are solved for by applying the two constraints.


First, the mean constraint \autoref{eq:mean_constraint_f} is applied to the assumed transformation:
\begin{align}
\sum_{j} P_j f_j' &= 1 \nonumber \\
\sum_{j} P_j (a \cdot f_j + b) &= 1 \nonumber \\
a \sum_{j} P_j f_j + b \sum_{j} P_j &= 1
\end{align}
Given that $\sum P_j = 1$ and $\sum P_j f_j = E[f] = 1$, the expression simplifies:
\begin{align}
a(1) + b(1) &= 1 \nonumber \\
 b &= 1 - a
\end{align}
Substituting this result back into \autoref{eq:linear_assumption} gives the transformation in terms of a single constant, $a$:
\begin{equation}
f_j' = a \cdot f_j + (1-a)
\label{eq:f_prime_with_a}
\end{equation}


Next, the variance preservation constraint is used to solve for $a$. First, the constraint $\var(\sigma') = \var(\sigma)$ is expressed in terms of $f$ and $f'$:
\begin{align}
\var(f' \avg{\sigma'}) &= \var(f \avg{\sigma}) \nonumber \\
\avg{\sigma'}^2 \var(f') &= \avg{\sigma}^2 \var(f) \nonumber \\
\var(f') &= \left(\frac{\avg{\sigma}}{\avg{\sigma'}}\right)^2 \var(f)
\label{eq:var_requirement}
\end{align}
This equation defines the \textit{required} variance for the new factors $f_j'$.

Now, the variance of the \textit{assumed} transformation (\autoref{eq:f_prime_with_a}) is calculated. Since $(1-a)$ is a constant, it does not affect the variance:
\begin{align}
\var(f') &=\var(a \cdot f + (1-a)) \nonumber \\
&= \var(a \cdot f) \nonumber \\
&= a^2 Var(f)
\label{eq:var_of_transform}
\end{align}

By equating the required variance (\autoref{eq:var_requirement})with the variance of the transformation (\autoref{eq:var_of_transform}), the constant $a$ can be solved:
\begin{align}
a^2 Var(f) &= \left(\frac{\avg{\sigma}}{\avg{\sigma'}}\right)^2 Var(f) \nonumber \\
a^2 &= \left(\frac{\avg{\sigma}}{\avg{\sigma'}}\right)^2 \nonumber \\
\implies a &= \frac{\avg{\sigma}}{\avg{\sigma'}}
\end{align}

Substituting the solved constants $a$ and $b = 1 - a$ back into the original linear assumption ( \autoref{eq:linear_assumption}) yields the final, variance-preserving transformation rule:
\begin{equation}
f_j' = \left(\frac{\avg{\sigma}}{\avg{\sigma'}}\right) f_j + \left[1 - \left(\frac{\avg{\sigma}}{\avg{\sigma'}}\right)\right]
\label{eq:f_prime_final}
\end{equation}
This formula provides the correct normalization factors $f_j'$ that satisfy both the mean and variance constraints.

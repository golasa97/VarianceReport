\begin{abstract}
Properly accounting for resonance self-shielding in the Unresolved Resonance Region (URR) is critical for accurate neutron transport simulation. The standard method relies on probability tables (p-tables) to represent the statistical variance of the cross-section. This paper identifies a methodological flaw in the standard p-table generation and application pipeline, wherein a decoupling of mean cross-section values leads to a non-physical scaling of the intended variance. This scaling introduces significant errors in self-shielding calculations. We derive a robust linear transformation to correct this flaw, ensuring the scaled probability distribution preserves the original, physically-sampled variance while matching the required infinitely-dilute mean. This corrected methodology is validated against simple transmission problems and benchmark applications for ${}^{235}$U, demonstrating a complete correction of the self-shielding error.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

The energy-dependent structure of neutron cross-sections is categorized into distinct regions. In between the Resolved Resonance Region (RRR) and the continuum region lies the Unresolved Resonance Region (URR), where cross-sections possess resonance structure, but individual resonances cannot be resolved experimentally. URR evaluations are thus limited to average cross-sections and average resonance parameters. This representation, however, omits the large variance in the cross-section's magnitude, which introduces a critical non-linear relationship between the average cross-section and the average neutron transmission. This effect is known as resonance self-shielding.

The standard method to account for self-shielding is the use of probability tables. These tables provide a discrete probability distribution of cross-section values, generated by statistically sampling many resonance "ladders" to reflect the underlying variance. It is critical that this variance is preserved.

This paper identifies a loss of variance preservation arising from a subtle decoupling in the standard methodology. Probability tables are generated by normalizing sampled resonance ladders against their own statistical average cross-section, but transport codes often sample from these tables using a different average cross-section (the smooth, infinitely-dilute value). When a discrepancy exists between these two averages, the variance is inadvertently scaled, leading to incorrect self-shielding. This paper will demonstrate this problem, derive a corrective methodology, and validate it using ${}^{235}$U test cases.

\section{Background}
\label{sec:background}

To clarify the notation used throughout this section, the following terms are defined. The true, unresolved cross-section is denoted by $\sigma$, which is statistically sampled to generate resonance ladders; its Monte Carlo samples are $\sigma_i$ with a sample mean of $\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}$. The deterministic, infinitely-dilute cross-section from the nuclear data evaluation (e.g., File 3) is denoted by $\sigma_{eval}(E)$, and its bin-averaged value is $\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}$. Finally, the cross-section values from the probability table, as used in the transport code, are denoted by $\sigma_{PT}$.

\subsection{Accounting for Self-Shielding}

For a known cross-section $\sigma(E)$, the true average transmission $\langle T \rangle_{true}$ over an energy bin $\Delta E$ is:
\begin{equation}
\langle T \rangle_{true} = \frac{1}{\Delta E} \int_{E_1}^{E_2} e^{-n\sigma(E)} dE
\label{eq:true_T}
\end{equation}
In the URR, only the average cross-section, $\langle \sigma \rangle$, is known. By adding and subtracting $\langle \sigma \rangle$ in the exponent, the terms can be separated:

\begin{align}
\langle T \rangle_{true} &= \frac{1}{\Delta E} \int_{E_1}^{E_2} e^{-n[\sigma(E) - \langle \sigma \rangle + \langle \sigma \rangle]} dE \\
&= e^{-n\langle \sigma \rangle} \left[ \frac{1}{\Delta E} \int_{E_1}^{E_2} e^{-n[\sigma(E) - \langle \sigma \rangle]} dE \right]
\label{eq:sep_T}
\end{align}
The $e^{-n\langle \sigma \rangle}$ term is the transmission based on the simple average. The integral term is the self-shielding contribution, $C_T$, which accounts for the variance.

\subsection{Background on Direct Sampling}
The self-shielding factor $C_T$ can be approximated via Monte Carlo integration:
\begin{equation}
C_T = \frac{1}{\Delta E} \int_{E_1}^{E_2} e^{-n[\sigma(E) - \langle \sigma \rangle]} dE \approx \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^{N} e^{-n[\sigma_i - \langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}]}
\label{eq:CT_mc}
\end{equation}
where $\sigma_i$ are cross-sections sampled from $N$ random resonance ladders, and $\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}$ is the statistical average of that sample set. The Monte Carlo averaged transmission is $\langle T \rangle_{MC}$. These are used to solve for $C_T$:
\begin{equation}
\frac{\langle T \rangle_{MC}}{e^{-n\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}} \approx C_T
\label{eq:CT_solve}
\end{equation}
This $C_T$ term accurately captures the variance. However, $\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}$ may not be the best representation of the true infinitely-dilute average. Therefore, a different value, $\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}$, is substituted to get the final "ground truth" transmission:
\begin{equation}
\langle T \rangle_{true} \approx e^{-n\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}} C_T
\label{eq:T_ground_truth}
\end{equation}

By combining these two components:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item The transmission of the smooth average: $\exp{(-n \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval})}$
    \item The self-shielding due to cross-section variance: $C_T$
\end{enumerate}
Then the self-shielded transmission is accurately approximated from average parameters and the average cross-section.

\subsection{Background on P-Table Calculations}
Transport codes cannot perform this sampling on the fly. Instead, p-tables are generated from the $N$ samples. These tables store probabilities $P_j$ and normalization factors $f_j$:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:f_def}
    f_j = \frac{\sigma_j}{\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}
\end{equation}
In the transport code, these factors are then used to construct the p-table cross-sections, $\sigma_{PT,j}$, by scaling with the "correct" infinitely-dilute cross-section, $\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}$:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:sigma_prime}
    \sigma_{PT,j} = f_j \times \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}
\end{equation}
This ensures the mean of the sampled distribution is correct:
\begin{align}
    \langle \sigma_{PT} \rangle 
        &= \sum P_j \sigma_{PT,j} \nonumber \\
        &= \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval} \sum P_j f_j \nonumber \\
        &= \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval} \nonumber
\end{align}

\subsection{The Flaw: Unintended Variance Scaling}
This scaling procedure has an unintended consequence. The variance of the new distribution, $\text{Var}(\sigma_{PT})$, is scaled. This is shown by propagating the constant factor multiplicative property of variance, $\text{Var}(aX) = a^2 \text{Var}(X)$:
\begin{align}
\text{Var}(\sigma_{PT}) &= \text{Var}(f_j \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}) \\
&= \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}^2 \text{Var}(f_j) \\
&= \langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}^2 \text{Var}\left(\frac{\sigma}{\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}\right) \\
&= \left(\frac{\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}}{\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}\right)^2 \text{Var}(\sigma)
\label{eq:var_scaling}
\end{align}
This equation shows that as $\frac{\langle \sigma \rangle_{eval}}{\langle \sigma \rangle_{MC}}$ diverges from 1, the variance of the cross-section seen by the transport code varies by the square of that fraction, and the impact from self-shielding is incorrectly estimated.

\section{Variance-Preserving P-Table Generation}
\label{sec:theory}

This section describes a procedure for constructing probability tables (p-tables) whose sampled cross sections reproduce a prescribed mean while preserving the physically sampled variance. The goal is to ensure consistency between unresolved resonance region (URR) probability tables and the corresponding pointwise (File 3) evaluation, without introducing artificial variance amplification or suppression.

\subsection{Mean Correction with Variance Preservation}
\label{ssec:var-pres}

$\{\sigma_{j}\}$ denotes the cross-section value sampled from a probability table within a given energy bin, with an associated probability $P_{j}$. The Monte Carlo sampled mean and variance are
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:mc-sampled-xs}
    \avg{\sigma}_{MC} = \sum_{j}P_{j}\sigma_{j}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:mc-sampled-variance}
    \var_{MC}(\sigma) = \sum_{j}P_{j} \left( \sigma_{j} - \avg{\sigma}_{MC} \right)^{2} 
\end{equation}
respectively.

$\avg{\sigma'}$ denotes a target infinitely dilute mean cross-section obtained from an evaluation (e.g., the averaged File 3 cross-section). The goal is to determine a transformed set $\{ \sigma'_{j} \}$ which satisfies:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:mc-sampled-xs-corrected}
    \avg{\sigma'} = \sum_{j}P_{j}\sigma'_{j} 
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:mc-sampled-variance-corrected}
    \var(\sigma') = \var_{MC}(\sigma)
\end{equation}

Utilizing the property that variance is constant under additive transformations,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:affine-shift}
    \sigma'_{j} = \sigma_{j} + \Delta
\end{equation}
where
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:affine-constant}
    \Delta = \avg{\sigma'} - \avg{\sigma}_{MC}
\end{equation}
As a result,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:const-var}
    \var(\sigma'_{j}) = \var(\sigma_{j})
\end{equation}

This can also be represented as the probability table factor as:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:ptable-affine}
    f'_{j} = \frac{\sigma_{j} + \avg{\sigma'} - \avg{\sigma}_{MC}}{\avg{\sigma'}}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Addressing ``Double Dipping''}
\label{ssec:double-dipping}

In \autoref{eq:const-var}, it's assumed that the entire variance in the cross-section should be attributable to the fluctuations in the probability table. However, It could be desireable to combine P-Table sampling with a fluctuating pointwise cross-section $\sigma_{3}(E)$, in which \textit{some} resonant structure is resolvable, but there is still measurable self-shielding when compared to simulations that must be accounted for. As a result, $\var{(\sigma_{3})}$ would be a non-negligible quantity, and including it would overestimate the true self-shielding. Therefore, the total variance at any given energy must be split into two separate components:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:variance-split-requirement}
    \var(\sigma) = \var(\sigma_{PT}) + \var(\sigma_{3})
\end{equation}
in which $\var(\sigma)$ is the true variance in the cross-section, and $\var(\sigma_{PT}$ is the remaining variance that $\var(\sigma_{3})$ does not account for.

This expression neglects a covariance term between $\sigma_{PT}$ and $\sigma_{3}$: $2\mathrm{Cov}(\sigma_{PT},\sigma_{3})$ which is assumed to be negligible. In practice, determining this covariance term would require knowledge of which resonant structures are being represented by $\sigma_{3}$ and which are being represented by $\sigma_{PT}$, and to what extent. However, by virtue of this information coming from unresolved resonances, sorting out the $2\mathrm{Cov}(\sigma_{PT}, \sigma_{3})$ term would significantly complicate this procedure without any significant gain.

The goal is therefore to determine a $\var(\sigma_{PT})$ such that
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:var-pt}
    \var(\sigma_{PT}) = \var(\sigma) - \var(\sigma_{3})
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:var-pt-xs-j}
    \var(\sigma_{PT}) = \var_{\sigma'_{j}}
\end{equation}
for some scaled set of probability table cross-sections $\{\sigma'_{j}\}$.

Linearly scaling the cross-section tables from the sampled $\{\sigma_{j}\}$ gives the form
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:linear-transform}
    \sigma'_{j} = a \cdot \sigma_{j} + b
\end{equation}
This means that $\var(\sigma'_{j})$ is equivalent to
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:step2}
    \var(\sigma'_{j}) = \var(a \cdot \sigma_{j} + b) = a^{2} \var(\sigma_{j})
\end{equation}
It is assumed that the true variance $\var(\sigma)$ is accurately represented by the originally sampled probability tables, i.e.,
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:assumption}
    \var(\sigma) = \var(\sigma_{j})
\end{equation}
Combining this assumption with \autoref{eq:step2} and \autoref{eq:variance-split-requirement}:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:step3}
    a^{2} \var(\sigma) = \var(\sigma) - \var(\sigma_{3})
\end{equation}
And solving for $a$:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:a-val}
    a = \sqrt{\frac{\var(\sigma) - \var(\sigma_{3})}{\var(\sigma)}}
\end{equation}

The $b$ term is used to solve the requirement such that the mean value sampling $\sigma'_{j}$ is equal to $\sigma_{3}$:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:req}
    \sigma_{3} = \sum_{j} P_{j} \left( a \sigma_{j} + b \right)
\end{equation}
Solving this sum produces
\begin{equation}
    a \avg{\sigma}_{MC} + b = \sigma_{3}
\end{equation}
and solving for $b$:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:b-solve}
    b = \sigma_{3} - a \avg{\sigma}_{MC}
\end{equation}
Filling $a$ and $b$ into \autoref{eq:linear-transform} solves for $\sigma'_{j}$ as:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:sigma-prime-clean}
    \sigma'_{j} = \sigma_{3} + \left( \sqrt{1 - \frac{\var(\sigma_{3})}{\var(\sigma)}} \right) \left( \sigma_{j} - \avg{\sigma}_{MC} \right)
\end{equation}
As a probability table factor, this ends up equating to:
\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:p-table-general}
    f'_{j} = \frac{\sigma'_{j}}{\sigma_{3}} = 1 + \frac{a}{\sigma_{3}} \left( \sigma_{j}  - \avg{\sigma}_{MC}\right)
\end{equation}

When probability tables in this form are used, then double dipping is no longer a concern.

An important limiting case is when the variance of the pointwise cross section vanishes, $\var(\sigma_{3}) \rightarrow 0$. In this case, the $a$ term approaches $1$, and consequently,  \autoref{eq:p-table-general} reduces to 
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:a-approaches-1}
    f'_{j} &= 1 + \frac{1}{\sigma_{3}} \left( \sigma_{j}  - \avg{\sigma}_{MC}\right) \\
           &= \frac{\sigma_{3} +  \left( \sigma_{j}  - \avg{\sigma}_{MC}\right) }{\sigma_{3}} \nonumber 
\end{align}
which is equivalent to the previous zero-variance form, \autoref{eq:ptable-affine}.

The other case is where both $\var(\sigma_{3})$ approaches 0, and $\sigma_{3}$ approaches $\avg{\sigma}_{MC}$. In this case, \autoref{eq:p-table-general} reduces such that:
\begin{align}
    \label{eq:reduced-case-2}
    f'_{j} &= 1 + \frac{1}{\avg{\sigma}_{MC}} \left( \sigma_{j} - \avg{\sigma}_{MC}\right) \\
           &= \frac{\avg{\sigma}_{MC} + \sigma_{j} - \avg{\sigma}_{MC}}{\avg{\sigma_{MC}}} \nonumber \\
           &= \frac{\sigma_{j}}{\avg{\sigma}_{MC}} \nonumber
\end{align}
which is equivalent to the original P-Table factor, shown in \autoref{eq:f_def}.

\autoref{eq:reduced-case-2} demonstrates why the original probability table factor method is generally accepted; most current evaluations have negligible $\var(\sigma_{3})$ quantities throughout the URR, and negligible difference between $\sigma_{3}$ and $\avg{\sigma}_{MC}$ as calculated throughout the probability tables.

This method will enable a more flexible utilization of the unresolved resonance region in the future.

\section{Implementation and Methodology}

This section documents the calculations performed in this study and the specific processing choices used to connect the theoretical development in 

This section connects the theoretical developments in \autoref{sec:background} and \autoref{sec:theory} to two applications. The first is a practical implementation of the $\var(\sigma_{3}=0$ limiting case into NJOY for generating variance-preserved probability tables. The second is a simulated transmission test of the general variance-budgeting form using a finely resolved pointwise $\sigma_{3}$ which contains significant variance.

\subsection{Criticality Benchmark and Rationale}
The full variance-budgeting form in \autoref{ssec:double-dipping} depends on redistributing the variance between the probability table contribution and a fluctuating $\sigma_{3}$. In practice, however, propagating a bin-dependent scaling of the probability-table distribution through general-purpose nuclear-data processing and sampling interfaces is difficult to implement without format- and code-level changes. 

This limitation does not materially restrict the $^{235}$U application studied here. In the ENDF-8.1 evaluation of $^{235}$U, the URR File 3 cross-section is very smooth, and therefore its $\var(\sigma_{3})$ is negligible. The dominant effect of changing probability-table treatment arises from enforcing mean consistency while preserving the probability-table variance.

Integral transport calculations are performed with MCNP. The HEU-MET-INTERM-011 critical benchmark was utilized, which is a $^{235}$U benchmark designed for URR sensitivity.

Each benchmark is run twice. Firstly, once with the uncorrected $^{235}$U ACE file, and the second using the corrected, variance preserved form. All other isotopes were used with the original probability table method in order to limit the extent of the impact to solely modifying $^{235}$U.


\subsection{General Method Transmission Demonstration}
A separate, controlled transmission example is used to test the general variance-budgeting method of \autoref{ssec:double-dipping} by using a $\sigma_{3}$ with an intentionally fine, highly resonant structure. The goal is to verify that the method actually preserves variance while allowing finer pointwise structure to coexist consistently with probability-table sampling.

To test this, a fine pointwise $\sigma_{3}$ is obtained from a highly enriched experiment[CITE HARVEY] and used over the same coarse binning used for transmission evaluation. The method is then assessed under two key expectations.

The first is that the coarse transmission remains invariant under $\sigma_{3}$ refinement. When the fine $\sigma_{3}$ cross-section is collapsed back into coarse bins, the transmission predicted by the variance-budgeted method should reproduce the transmission obtained using the corresponding coarse representation. In other words, refining $\sigma_{3}$ within a bin should not change the bin-averaged transmission, so long as the bin-mean and variance are preserver.

The second is that the fine structure of $\sigma_{3}$ should be observable in transmission. This would indicate that this method is able to influence the flux profile in a desireable way.

Combining these two expectations shows that including fine structure in the URR not only \emph{can} be done, but also \emph{should} be done in instances where coupled flux profiles are important [SOMETHING ABOUT U-235 AND PLUTONIUM]
